Pasteurization

THEN

Microbiologist Louis Pasteur (1822-1895) is best known for discovering that microorganisms develop during fermentation. He invented pasteurization, a process to make liquids safe to drink by heating them to kill bacteria and molds.

NOW

Pasteurization has revolutionized food safety and the way the world drinks.
But...

1
2
3

Raw milk advocates claim key health benefits are destroyed during pasteurization, which scientists dispute.

Reproductive Health

THEN

In 1960, Enovid, the first oral contraceptive pill, hit the market and plastic condoms came out in 1995. In 2000, four new birth control products were approved by the FDA—the first in a decade.

NOW

Contraceptives transformed women's lives globally and revolutionized the modern women’s rights movement.
But...

1
2
3

In 2007, the first HPV (a sexually transmitted disease) vaccine to prevent cervical cancer was created. Given to women between 11 and 26, the vaccine is criticized as condoning sexual activity instead of abstinence.

Vaccinations

THEN

Edward Jenner (1749-1843) published a paper in 1796 about smallpox inoculation, which led to the first vaccine. The International Smallpox Eradication Program was established 170 years later and through mass immunization, the disease was wiped out in the Americas in the 1950s.

NOW

Massive global efforts for vaccination brought worldwide eradication of smallpox in 1977.
But...

1
2
3

Parents are claiming nonmedical exemptions from vaccinations, leaving their children—and other children in their communities—vulnerable to diseases. There is heated disagreement about vaccines and their connection to autism.

Stem Cell Research

THEN

DNA’s double helix model was first published by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1954. In 1990, the Human Genome Project endeavored to map our DNA down to the nucleotide (or base pair) level, which was completed in 13 years.

NOW

Invaluable practical uses resulted even before the research was complete, including insights to human disease.
But...

1
2
3

Many say it’s wrong to use or clone human stem cells, which can grow indefinitely into any the 220 types of cells found in the human body.

Toilets

THEN

In the 1920s, the first flushing toilets with indoor plumbing became widespread in American homes, revolutionizing public hygiene and safety.

NOW

Toilets are ubiquitous in most developed countries.
But...

1
2
3

Work is still needed to produce toilets that are more practical to the 40% of the world where toilets and access to water, sewers, and electricity are scarce.

HIV/AIDS

THEN

HIV/AIDS gained a lot of attention in the 1980s, but stigma still exists around the world in a variety of ways, including ostracism, discrimination; compulsory testing without prior consent and involuntary quarantine.

NOW

While there’s no cure, anti-retroviral treatments have been developed, allowing many infected individuals to lead healthy lives.
But...

1
2
3

Cultural stigma prevents people from seeking testing and treatment.

Public Healthcare

THEN

American Lillian Wald (1867- 1940), a nurse, social worker and feminist, introduced the pioneering concept of "public health nursing," which placed medical care within the reach of the poor. Wald established a nursing insurance partnership with Met Life Insurance that became a model for many other corporate projects and proposed that America adopt a national health insurance plan.

NOW

Today, America’s Affordable Care Act began enrollments on October 1, 2013.
But...

1
2
3

Nationalized healthcare remains one of the most heated, divisive issues in the country, with the government shutting down for two weeks in October as politicians attempted to stop implementation of the Affordable Care Act.

Antibiotics

THEN

In 1928, Scottish physician Alexander Fleming (1881-1955) inadvertently discovered penicillin, the first antibiotic. This discovery that antibiotics can fight infectious disease would be one of the most important of the 20th Century.

NOW

Saving countless lives, antibiotics are some of the most effective weapons we have to stop bacterial diseases.
But...

1
2
3

“Superbugs” that are resistant to antibiotics are a growing, man-made problem caused by overprescribing antibiotics, drug misuse, and patients not completing their treatments.

Quarantines

THEN

With the mass influx of immigrants to American shores during the 19th and 20th Centuries, the government mandated health quarantines. To prevent the spread of infectious diseases, these involuntary quarantines required mandatory health inspections and treatments, including spraying people en masse with disinfectant.

NOW

Identifying and treating infectious individuals helps prevent widespread outbreaks.
But...

1
2
3

International travel makes it easier to spread pathogens quickly. But many protest that regulations cross the line with mandatory health tests, detaining or fining travelers, and demanding personal health histories.

EPA

THEN

In 1970, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) was established under the Nixon Administration in response to growing public concern that legislative policies didn’t take into account environmental health and safety. The EPA is responsible for protecting human health and with safeguarding air, water, and land.

NOW

The EPA has the power to enforce federal regulation standards.
But...

1
2
3

It’s unknown how pollution is related to chronic illness, asthma, cancer, and death and many argue the EPA either does too little (or too much) to safeguard environmental and human health

Malaria

THEN

There are an estimated 660,000 deaths annually from malaria, mostly among African children. 219 million people contract it annually. Bug sprays are recommended to prevent mosquito-born diseases like malaria, and most contain DEET, a chemical first developed by the U.S. military for personnel.

NOW

DEET is considered one of the few insect repellents that are truly effective.
But...

1
2
3

Due to the extremely high cost of malaria prevention medication, many opt to risk exposure, since the cost of malaria treatment is often less expensive.
